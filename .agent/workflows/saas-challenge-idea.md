# SaaS Idea Challenge & Validation

## Phase 1: Gather the Idea

**If idea not provided**, ask the user:

```
Tell me about your idea:
- What problem does it solve?
- Who is it for?
- What's your proposed solution?
- What makes it different?
```

**Do NOT proceed** until you understand:
- The specific problem being solved
- The target user/customer
- The proposed solution
- Any claimed differentiation

---

## Phase 2: Research Competitors

**Use web searches** to find existing solutions:
- Search: "[problem] tool", "[solution] software", "[target user] platform"
- Check: Product Hunt, Y Combinator portfolio, Crunchbase
- Search: Reddit discussions, Alternative.to, Capterra, G2

**For each competitor found**, document:

### Competitor Profile
- **Name and URL**
- **What they do** (1-2 sentences)
- **Target market** (who they serve)
- **Key features** (list 5-7 main capabilities)
- **Pricing** (tiers and approximate costs)
- **Traction indicators**:
  - Funding raised (if available)
  - Team size (LinkedIn)
  - Social media following
  - Reviews/ratings (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
  - Traffic estimates (SimilarWeb, if available)
- **Founded date** (how long they've been around)

**Aim to find at least 5-10 competitors**. Cast a wide net:
- Direct competitors (same solution)
- Indirect competitors (different solution, same problem)
- Adjacent tools (different problem, same audience)
- Enterprise vs. SMB alternatives

---

## Phase 3: Brutally Honest Analysis

### 3.1 Market Reality Check

Answer these questions with **evidence, not optimism**:

**Q1: Is this problem actually painful enough?**
- Are people paying for existing solutions? (Check competitor pricing pages)
- Are people complaining about it online? (Reddit, Twitter, forums)
- How much time/money does this problem cost users?
- **Red flag**: If no one is paying for solutions, the problem may not be painful enough.

**Q2: Is the market crowded or wide open?**
- How many well-funded competitors exist?
- Are there established players with strong moats?
- Is this a mature market or emerging one?
- **Red flag**: 10+ established competitors means very hard to break in.
- **Red flag**: Zero competitors might mean no market demand.

**Q3: What's the claimed differentiation - is it real?**

**Real differentiation looks like:**
- Unique data/network effects (gets better with more users)
- Proprietary technology (patents, unique algorithms)
- Distribution advantage (existing audience, partnerships)
- Regulatory moat (licenses, compliance)
- Specific niche positioning (underserved segment)

### 3.2 Competitive Threat Assessment

For each major competitor, assess:

**How hard would it be for them to add your features?**
- If the answer is "easy" or "a few sprints", your idea is not defensible.

**What would you need to do 10x better to get users to switch?**
- Switching costs are high. Incremental improvement is not enough.

### 3.3 Market Entry Challenges

**Be honest about:**
- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**: How will you reach users affordably?
- **Sales cycle**: How long to close a deal? (B2B can be 6-12 months)
- **Minimum viable product**: How much needs to be built before it's useful?
- **Regulatory/compliance**: Any legal hurdles? (HIPAA, GDPR, SOC2, etc.)
- **Integrations needed**: Do you need to integrate with Salesforce, Slack, etc. to be viable?

## Phase 5: Specific Recommendations

**Regardless of verdict, provide:**

### If Proceeding:

1. **Who to target FIRST** (most underserved segment)
2. **Minimum feature set** (what's the smallest viable product?)
3. **Key validation steps** (before building anything):
   - [ ] Talk to 20+ potential users
   - [ ] Get 50+ email signups from landing page
   - [ ] Test pricing willingness (not "would you pay?" but "which tier?")
4. **Biggest risks to watch** (what could kill this?)
5. **Unfair advantage needed** (what moat can you build?)

### If Stopping:

1. **Why the market isn't ready** or **why incumbents are too strong**
2. **Adjacent opportunities** that might be better
3. **Lessons to apply to next idea**

## Tone & Approach

- **Be brutally honest** - False hope helps no one
- **Use evidence** - Link to competitor sites, reviews, funding data
- **Acknowledge uncertainty** - But make a call based on available data
- **Be specific** - Vague advice like "focus on marketing" is useless
- **Show your work** - Explain the reasoning behind the verdict
- **Empathize but don't sugarcoat** - It's better to kill a bad idea early

**Remember**: The goal is not to crush dreams, but to save founders from wasting years on doomed ideas. A harsh truth today is kinder than failure later.
